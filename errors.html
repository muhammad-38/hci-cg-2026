Common Mistakes & Solutions

During the first 4 weeks, I identified these 10 errors. Correcting them improved the Gulf of Evaluation for users relying on assistive technology.

Analysis of 10 HTML Structural Errors
# The Mistake (Incorrect) The Fix (Semantic/Correct) HCI Rationale
1 <b>Main Title</b> <h1>Main Title</h1> Dix (p. 209) emphasizes navigation structure. Screen readers use H1-H6 to build a map for the user.
2 <img src="btn.png"> <img src="btn.png" alt="Submit"> Missing alt text creates a "Gulf of Evaluation" for visually impaired users.
3 <nav> <a>...</a> </nav> <nav><ul><li><a>...</a></li></ul></nav> Semantic lists tell screen readers exactly how many menu items exist.
4 <div id="nav"> (Twice) Use class="nav" or unique IDs Duplicate IDs break the Document Object Model (DOM) and make interaction unpredictable.
5 <font color="red"> <style> color: red; </style> Separation of concerns. Presentation (CG) should not be mixed with structure (HTML).
6 <a href="#">Click Here</a> <a href="cv.html">View My CV</a> "Click here" is vague. Descriptive links reduce the user's "Mental Preparation" (M-operator) time.
7 <label>Name:</label> <input> <label for="n">Name:</label> <input id="n"> Without the 'for' attribute, the label isn't clickable, increasing the difficulty of "Pointing" (P-operator).
8 <table><tr><td>Header</td>... <table><thead><tr><th>... Using th with scope is vital for tabular data accessibility.
9 Skipping <h2> and going to <h3> Maintain strict h1 -> h2 -> h3 hierarchy Broken hierarchy confuses the user's mental model of the page content.
10 Missing <!DOCTYPE html> Include <!DOCTYPE html> at top Prevents "Quirks Mode," ensuring the "Computer" side of the interaction renders pixels correctly.
